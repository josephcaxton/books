# Chapter 11: Cultivating Problem Framers

## Part IV: The Future-Proof Leader

## The Executive Vignette

A global management consultancy hired two junior associates in the same cohort. Both held degrees from top-tier universities. Both had strong academic records. Both passed the same case interview process.

Associate A was given a client engagement: a mid-market retailer struggling with declining in-store conversion rates. She produced a 47 slide deck in six days — meticulously researched, beautifully formatted, and packed with granular data on foot traffic patterns, demographic shifts, competitor pricing, and digital marketing benchmarks. The deck was objectively impressive as a piece of research execution.

The client's CEO reviewed it and said: "This is very thorough. But what is the actual problem we are solving? I still do not know where to focus."

Associate B received a different engagement: a logistics company with rising delivery costs. She produced a single page document in two days. It contained three sentences defining the problem — not describing it, not researching it, but structurally isolating the root mechanism causing cost escalation. Below those three sentences, she had written four questions that, if answered, would confirm or deny her hypothesis. The document concluded with a recommended sequence of investigation.

The client's COO read it and said: "This is exactly what we needed. We have been drowning in data for months. No one had given us the structural question."

Associate A was an excellent executor. She could gather information, organise it, and present it with precision. But her output, however thorough, did not advance the client toward a decision. It gave them more material to process, not more clarity about what to do.

Associate B was a problem framer. She did not produce volume. She produced structure. She took a complex, ambiguous situation and reduced it to its load bearing components. The specific structural logic that, once understood, made the solution path obvious.

In a world where AI systems can produce research, generate analyses, write reports, and process information faster than any human, execution is no longer the scarce commodity. The scarce commodity is the ability to define what problem is actually being solved.

## The Strategic Framework

### 11.1 The Death of Execution Value: Why Output Generation Is No Longer Premium

For decades, the corporate talent market has rewarded execution skill. The associate who could produce a polished deck faster. The analyst who could build a complex model overnight. The lawyer who could draft a contract from precedent in a single sitting. Speed and volume of output determined career velocity.

This premium is collapsing.

When AI systems can generate a first draft research report in 90 seconds, summarise a 200 page document in 30 seconds, produce financial models from natural language specifications, and draft legal documents from template logic — the value of human output generation drops asymptotically toward zero for any output type that follows a pattern.

**What remains expensive:**

| Capability | AI Performance | Human Premium |
|---|---|---|
| Raw information gathering | Excellent — faster and more comprehensive than any human | Near zero |
| Pattern based drafting (reports, emails, documents) | Excellent — adequate quality at extreme speed | Near zero |
| Data analysis and visualisation | Excellent — faster, more consistent | Low |
| Summarisation and compression | Excellent — superior to average human | Low |
| **Defining which question to ask** | Poor — requires understanding of unstated goals, political context, and strategic intent | **Very High** |
| **Isolating root cause from symptom** | Poor — requires judgment about what matters vs what merely exists | **Very High** |
| **Structuring ambiguous situations into solvable logic** | Poor — requires creative abstraction and domain wisdom | **Very High** |
| **Determining what is strategically relevant** | Poor — requires understanding of business context, stakeholder dynamics, and time horizons | **Very High** |

The bottom four rows describe the core capability of a Problem Framer. These are the cognitive functions that remain permanently expensive because they require something AI systems structurally lack: the ability to determine what matters in contexts where the definition of "mattering" is itself ambiguous and unstated.

### 11.2 Anatomy of a Problem Framer: The Core Skill Set

A Problem Framer is not simply a "strategic thinker" — a term so broad as to be meaningless in a hiring context. The capability is specific, observable, and measurable. It consists of four distinct cognitive skills:

**Skill 1: Root Cause Isolation**

The ability to distinguish the causal mechanism generating a problem from the symptoms the mechanism produces. Most people describe symptoms. Problem Framers diagnose mechanisms.

*Example:* "Our customer retention is declining" is a symptom. "Our onboarding workflow creates a 72 hour gap between purchase and first value delivery, during which 34% of customers cancel" is a root cause isolation. The first statement generates meetings. The second generates a solution.

**Skill 2: Semantic Precision**

The ability to articulate a problem in language so specific that it becomes immediately actionable — eliminating ambiguity, scope creep, and interpretation variance.

*Example:* "We need to improve our customer experience" is semantically empty — it means everything and therefore nothing. "We need to reduce the average resolution time for Tier 2 support escalations from 4.2 hours to under 90 minutes without increasing headcount" is semantically precise. A system can act on the second statement. It cannot act on the first.

**Skill 3: Structural Decomposition**

The ability to take a complex, multi-variable situation and break it into its constituent structural components, isolating the load bearing elements from the decorative ones.

*Example:* A company's revenue has plateaued. There are 50 possible contributing factors. A Problem Framer identifies that the plateau correlates precisely with the saturation of a single acquisition channel — and that all other variables are noise. The structural insight reduces a paralysing 50 variable problem to a single actionable decision: diversify the channel mix.

**Skill 4: Question Architecture**

The ability to design a sequence of questions that, when answered, will inevitably reveal the solution, even before the answers are known.

*Example:* Rather than immediately researching a complex market entry decision, a Problem Framer defines: "If we can confirm (a) the addressable market exceeds $40M annually, (b) no regulatory barrier exists for foreign operators, and (c) our existing product serves the top three use cases — then entry is justified. If any condition fails, it is not." The questions become the decision framework. The research becomes targeted rather than exhaustive.

### 11.3 The Interview and Evaluation Blueprint: How to Identify Problem Framers

Traditional hiring processes — resume screening, behavioural interviews, case study presentations, are designed to identify execution competence. They do not reliably identify framing ability. A candidate can be articulate, experienced, and impressive in a structured interview while possessing zero capacity for structural problem decomposition.

**Evaluation Criteria:**

| What to Assess | How to Assess | Red Flags |
|---|---|---|
| Root cause isolation | Present an ambiguous business scenario with multiple possible causes. Ask: "What is the structural mechanism causing this?" | Candidate lists symptoms rather than isolating a mechanism. Offers multiple possibilities without ranking or structuring. |
| Semantic precision | Ask the candidate to define a vague business objective in actionable terms. "What would you actually measure?" | Candidate reuses the vague language. Cannot convert aspiration into measurable target. |
| Structural decomposition | Present a complex, multi-factor situation. Ask: "Which elements are load bearing and which are noise?" | Candidate treats all factors as equally important. Cannot prioritise or eliminate. |
| Question architecture | Ask: "What three questions would you need answered to make this decision?" | Candidate lists information needs rather than decision criteria. Questions are research tasks, not structural gates. |

**Interview Format:**

Replace at least one traditional interview round with a "live framing exercise":

1. Present the candidate with a real (anonymised) business problem your organisation has faced — one where the answer is known but the framing was historically difficult.
2. Give them 15 minutes to think (silently, alone, with a whiteboard or paper).
3. Ask them to present: What is the structural problem? What three questions would confirm or deny your hypothesis? What would you do first?

Evaluate not the answer (which may differ from your historical solution) but the structure of their thinking. A strong Problem Framer will:
- Ask clarifying questions before diving in (testing assumptions).
- Eliminate irrelevant information deliberately.
- Produce a hypothesis within minutes rather than requesting more data indefinitely.
- Frame the problem in language a system could act on.

**What to Deprioritise:**

- Resume prestige (university, employer brand) — no correlation with framing ability.
- Years of experience — framing capability is not time dependent. A 26 year old can outframe a 20 year veteran.
- Presentation polish — Problem Framers often communicate in blunt structural language rather than elegant narrative. Do not penalise directness.

### 11.4 Building an Intellectual Sandbox: Designing a Culture of Rigorous Curiosity

Problem Framers do not emerge from compliance cultures. They emerge from environments that actively reward questioning, hypothesis testing, and intellectual honesty — even when the hypothesis challenges existing assumptions held by senior leadership.

**Design Principles for an Intellectual Sandbox:**

**Principle 1: Separate "Framing Time" from "Execution Time"**

In most organisations, the pressure to produce output is so intense that no one has time to think about whether they are solving the right problem. The result is highly efficient execution of the wrong thing.

Structurally protect framing time. Before any significant initiative begins execution, mandate a defined "framing sprint" — a period (typically 3 to 5 days) where the team's only job is to define the problem, not solve it. No decks. No research. No output. Just structural questions.

**Principle 2: Reward Kills as Highly as Wins**

In most cultures, stopping a project is a failure. In a framing culture, stopping a project because someone identified that the problem was wrong is a success, potentially more valuable than a project that ran to completion solving the wrong thing.

Publicly reward and celebrate the moments where rigorous framing prevented wasted execution. This signals that intellectual honesty is valued more than activity.

**Principle 3: Tolerate Productive Discomfort**

Problem Framers challenge assumptions. They ask uncomfortable questions. They say "I think we are solving the wrong problem" in rooms full of people who have already committed resources. This requires a culture that distinguishes productive challenge from insubordination.

Leadership must model this by publicly acknowledging when their own initial framing was wrong, demonstrating that being corrected is an improvement, not a threat.

**Principle 4: Make Framing Visible**

Most problem framing happens invisibly — in someone's head, in a private notebook, in a conversation that was never documented. Make it visible.

Require teams to document their problem frame explicitly before beginning any project. Publish it internally. Invite challenge. If no one can identify a flaw in the frame within 48 hours, proceed. If someone can, that person has just saved the organisation potentially months of misdirected execution.

## Executive Summary

> - The historic premium on execution speed and output volume is collapsing. AI systems generate research, analyses, reports, and drafts faster and cheaper than any human. Execution is no longer the scarce commodity.
> - The new premium is on Problem Framing — the ability to define what problem is actually being solved, isolate root causes from symptoms, and structure ambiguous situations into actionable logic that systems can execute against.
> - Problem Framers possess four specific skills: root cause isolation, semantic precision, structural decomposition, and question architecture. These capabilities are observable, assessable, and distinct from general "strategic thinking."
> - Traditional hiring processes do not identify framing ability. Replace at least one interview round with a live framing exercise that evaluates structure of thinking over polish of presentation.
> - Problem Framers emerge from cultures that protect framing time, reward project kills as highly as project wins, tolerate productive discomfort, and make problem definitions visible and challengeable.

## Monday Morning Action Items

1. **Audit your last three major projects for framing quality.** For each, ask: was the structural problem explicitly defined before execution began? Was the definition challenged or validated? If not — and execution proceeded directly from a vague brief — estimate the cost of misdirected effort. Present this to your leadership team as the "framing deficit."

2. **Redesign one interview round as a live framing exercise.** Select your next senior hire (any function) and replace one traditional interview with the format described in Section 11.3. Evaluate the candidate's ability to isolate root cause, eliminate noise, and define actionable questions — not their ability to present research or demonstrate experience.

3. **Introduce a mandatory "framing sprint" for your next initiative.** Before the next significant project enters execution, mandate a 3 to 5 day framing period where the team's only deliverable is a one page problem definition: what is the structural mechanism, what three questions confirm or deny the hypothesis, and what is the first action if confirmed. No research. No deck. Just structure.

4. **Update your internal talent metrics.** Work with your HR team to define and begin tracking "framing capability" as a measurable competency in performance reviews and succession planning — alongside traditional execution metrics. Identify the three strongest Problem Framers in your current organisation and document what distinguishes their approach.
