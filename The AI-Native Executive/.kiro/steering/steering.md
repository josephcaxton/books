# Editorial Steering Rules

## Tone and Style Matrix
- Voice: Authoritative, pragmatic, clear, and highly professional. Like a Harvard Business Review essay or a McKinsey global report.
- Language: Executive-level business vocabulary. Use terms like "unit economics," "inference costs," "agentic workflows," and "proprietary data moats."
- Pacing: High information density. Cut empty transitional filler words ("In today's fast-paced world...", "It is important to remember..."). Lead directly with insights.

## Formatting Hierarchy
Every chapter must adhere to this exact structural theme:
1. Executive Vignette/Case Study (Real-world corporate scenario)
2. Core Strategic Framework (Divided into 2-3 logical sub-sections)
3. "Executive Summary" Callout Box (Bullet points for rapid C-suite review)
4. Monday Morning Action Items (3-5 highly actionable operational steps)

## Typography & Grammar Constraints

### Rule: Hyphenation Minimization
- **Rule:** THE AGENT SHALL limit the use of hyphens to join words (compound modifiers) unless it is grammatically incorrect or alters the meaning to omit them.
- **Preferred Style (Open Compounds):** Write terms as open compounds (e.g., "decision making models", "supply chain adjustments", "risk management playbook", "real world scenarios") instead of hyphenated forms ("decision-making models", "supply-chain adjustments", "risk-management playbook", "real-world scenarios").
- **Exceptions:** Only use hyphens when absolutely necessary for structural prefixes (e.g., "AI-native", "non-technical", "co-author") or to avoid severe syntactic ambiguity.
