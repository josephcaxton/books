# Chapter 9: Time — The Only Currency You Cannot Earn Back

## 1. The Lesson I Was Forced to Learn

If you are born and die at seventy years of age, you will have lived approximately 613,200 hours.

By the time you are thirty, 262,980 of those hours are already gone. You are left with 350,220. Remove eight hours of sleep every night and another 116,800 disappear, leaving you with roughly 233,420 hours to build something that matters. And we have not yet accounted for the time spent eating, bathing, commuting, managing family difficulties, recovering from illness, or simply sitting in the noise of modern life.

The window to build something meaningful is far smaller than most people ever stop to calculate.

Time is the only resource you can use to buy things but can never buy back. Money lost can be earned again. Reputation damaged can be rebuilt. Even health, to a degree, can be restored. But an hour spent carelessly is gone with a finality that nothing else in life matches.

We all receive the same 24 hours. Yet somehow, some people manage to build companies, stay physically strong, read a book a week, and still be present for the people they love, while others reach 11 o'clock at night genuinely confused about where the day went. The difference is rarely talent. It is almost always attention specifically, the discipline to notice and plug the silent leaks that drain your hours before you realise they are gone.


## 2. The Stories of My Journey

### My Typical Day

I want to give you something concrete, so I will share how I structure my own time. I am not offering this as the only way. I am offering it as evidence that a system, consistently applied, changes what becomes possible in a day.

I wake up at 4am. The first three hours of my morning are not given to the distraction. They belong to preparation. I meditate. I pray. I read. By 7am, when I shower and dress, my mind has been deliberately prepared for what the day requires. I dress properly every morning, not casually, not loosely, but as though I am going to a corporate office, whether I am leaving the house or not. This is not vanity. Clothing signals your brain what mode you are in. When you dress like you are going to work, your energy rises to meet it.

I sit at my desk and go through my emails. Then I write my to-do list for the day. I separate administrative tasks from deep work tasks. I put the hardest task at the top of the list, deliberately. Most people do the opposite; they warm up with small tasks and build toward the difficult one. I have found this backwards. If I get the hardest thing done early, the energy I recover from that accomplishment carries me through the rest of the day. There is a specific return of momentum that comes from completing something important. I want to collect that return early.

I set alarms on my phone for every meeting of the day, with a two-minute warning before each one. This allows me to work in deep focus between meetings without using mental energy to monitor the clock. As soon as I am off a call, I note any actions required and return to deep work immediately. It takes between fifteen and twenty minutes for the mind to re-enter full focus after a meeting; that transition time is real and must be managed. On my desk I keep a Pomodoro timer cube. When I need to push myself back into deep concentration, I flip it to fifty minutes. The physical act of turning it on becomes a signal to my brain: we are working now.

Around 1pm to 2pm I take an hour break. When I return, I shift to tasks that do not require deep concentration. This is not laziness, it is energy management. I have more cognitive power in the morning than in the afternoon. Protecting my mornings for the hardest work and my afternoons for lighter tasks is a deliberate allocation, not a coincidence.

I eat once a day, at around 5pm to 6pm. I keep water, coffee, or lemon water on my desk throughout the day. In the evening I catch up on news and reading. Sometimes I return to my desk and work until 10pm. Before I sleep, I do what I call a self-standup, a brief review of what happened during the day. What was done. What was not. What needs to carry forward. Then I fall asleep listening to a podcast, an audiobook, or classical music.

That system did not arrive fully formed. It was built and refined over years of paying attention to when I work well and when I do not.

### The Gym

Every other day, I go to the gym. I spend about three hours each visit.

You may think that is too much time. Here is how it breaks down: one hour running, thirty minutes on weights and stretching, and an hour in the jacuzzi, steam room, and sauna, sometimes swimming. The rest is showering and changing. That level of commitment was not where I started. It was built gradually over years, as my body began to ask for more.

What the gym gives me is not only physical. I return from every session with more energy than I left with. Mental fog clears. And some of my best thinking happens during those hours, ideas surface. Problems untangle, decisions become clearer. The body and the mind are not separate systems. When one is moving, the other tends to follow.

When I was looking for the house I live in now, I made a deliberate decision to factor my work and health habits into the search. I needed an office in the home. I needed a high quality gym within easy reach, one with the full range of facilities I use. Both conditions were met. There is also a golf club ten-minute walk away. I do not play golf currently, but the option is there if I ever choose to start. I did not leave these things to chance. I chose my environment to support the life I wanted to live.


## 3. My Advice to the Next Generation

### Calculate Your Time — Then Take It Seriously

Sit down and do the arithmetic. Work out how many hours you actually have left, factoring in sleep, daily necessities, and the realistic demands of your life. Write the number down.

Most people live as though time is abundant, as though there is always more of it somewhere ahead. The calculation tends to correct that assumption quickly. I am not suggesting you live in anxiety about time. I am suggesting you live with a respect for it that most people never develop until it is too late.

### Find Your Own System — Then Protect It

I have shared my day not because you should copy it, but because you need one. A deliberate system, consistently applied, is the difference between a productive day and a reactive one.

The most important thing is that you identify how you personally enter the state of deep focus and then protect the conditions that allow you to get there. When you find yourself working well, pause and ask: *what led to this moment? What was present? What was absent?* If you can identify those conditions, you can reproduce them. That is the beginning of your system.

Pay attention to your energy across the day. Where is your peak? What depletes you quickly? What restores you? Not everyone is the same. I have more power in the morning. You may be different. Build your schedule around your biology, not around convention.

### Name and Plug Your Leaks

Time management does not fail because of large catastrophes. It fails through small, invisible habits that quietly consume hours before you notice they are gone.

Doomscrolling, picking up your phone to check a single notification and surfacing forty-five minutes later, having achieved nothing. Bouncing between tasks without completing any of them, forcing your brain to pay a focus tax every time it switches. Disguising procrastination as preparation, over-researching, reorganising, tidying up, anything that feels productive but is actually avoidance of the real work.

To find your leaks, run a time audit. For three days, track what you actually do in thirty minute blocks. Not what you plan to do. What you actually do. The gap between what you believe you spend your time on and what you actually spend your time on is almost always uncomfortable to look at. That discomfort is useful. It replaces vague guilt with specific information and specific information can be acted on.

Once you find your leaks, introduce friction. Put your phone in another room. Use an app blocker. Schedule your most important work into fixed time slots and treat them as non-negotiable. When a difficult task is in front of you and you feel the pull toward avoidance, use the five minute rule: commit to working on it for just five minutes. The resistance to starting is almost always larger than the resistance to continuing. Five minutes is usually enough to break through the wall.

### Put the Hardest Thing First

Every morning, identify the task that matters most and do it first. Not after the easy tasks.

The energy you recover from completing something important early in the day compounds forward. The weight you carry all day from the important thing you have been avoiding is a tax on every other hour. Eliminate the tax early.

### Think For Yourself

When someone gives you advice about how to live, what to eat, when to sleep, how to structure your day, always ask: *where does their interest sit?*

Most doctors will tell you that breakfast is the most important meal of the day. That advice does not work for my biology. After breakfast, I feel sluggish and lose focus. I eat once a day and I operate at my best. I am also not oblivious to the fact that breakfast cereal is one of the most profitable processed food categories in existence, with margins of 40% to 50%, and that the food industry has historically funded research that supports its own commercial interests.

I am not telling you to skip breakfast. I am telling you to test everything against your own experience. Advice that comes from someone with a financial interest in your behaviour deserves scrutiny, not automatic obedience. Think clearly. Experiment honestly. Keep what works for you. Discard what does not.

The point of time management is not to fill every hour with activity. It is to ensure that the hours you have, the genuinely limited, genuinely irreplaceable hours you have go toward building the life and legacy you were designed to create.

### Make Exercise Non-Negotiable

Exercise is not a luxury that fits into your schedule when everything else is done. It is a tool for maintaining the mental and physical capacity to do everything else well.

You do not need to start where I am. Start with a walk. Then register at a gym and go twice a week for thirty minutes. Do that consistently, and your body will begin to tell you when it needs more. Scale from there at your own pace. The important thing is that you begin, and that you treat it as a fixed part of your week, not an optional extra.

Exercise clears mental fog in a way that little else can. Ideas come. Decisions become clearer. The hours you invest in your physical health are returned to you many times over in the quality of your focus and thinking.

### Design Your Environment for the Life You Want to Live

When you are in a position to choose where you live, think beyond the obvious. The size of the property. The neighbourhood. The commute.

Think about the habits you want to sustain and the facilities that support them. Is there a good gym nearby? Green space for walking? The things that maintain your health and focus are not secondary considerations, they are infrastructure. Your environment will either make your best habits easy or make them hard. Choose accordingly.

I factored a home office and a nearby gym into my decision when I bought my current home. Those were not afterthoughts. They were requirements. Your health and your focus are too important to leave to inconvenience.

### Guard your time. It is the only thing that, once gone, cannot be returned to you at any price.
