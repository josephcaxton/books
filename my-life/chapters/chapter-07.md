# Chapter 7: Order as a Discipline

## 1. The Lesson I Was Forced to Learn

Paying attention to detail is not a passive trait you are born with. It is a cognitive muscle, and like every muscle, it must be trained.

For most of my early life, I did not know this. I could not see what I was missing because I had never been taught to look. I grew up in an environment where disorder was the norm, and when disorder is all you have ever known, you do not experience it as disorder. It simply feels like life.

What I was forced to learn, gradually, and sometimes humiliatingly is that the quality of your attention filters directly into the quality of everything you produce. Your thinking becomes your product. The details you miss become the problems your customers, your employers, and the people who depend on you eventually have to pay for.

Order is not a luxury reserved for certain types of people. It is a discipline that determines the standard of your work, the quality of your reputation, and ultimately, the size of what you are trusted to build.


## 2. The Stories of My Journey

### Two Houses

There are different kinds of families in Nigeria where I was born. If I were to put them into two categories, I would call them the organised and the disorganised. I came from the disorganised.

Our house was large and open to everyone. Different apartments, different people, some of them living with us without my parents' knowledge or consent. Extended family members depending on the household for accommodation, food, and schooling. House maids. Half-brothers. Half-sisters. People I could not account for, who had simply appeared one day and stayed. Untrained dogs running freely through every room. All doors open. All boundaries fluid. The household was, at any given moment, a small community, loud, warm, generous, and in permanent comfortable chaos.

In that house, everything is out of order.

Some of my friends came from a completely different world, even though their houses were just as large, or larger. The number of people living inside was four or five, maximum. No strangers were permitted. All doors were locked. The security men and the gardeners worked outside and stayed outside. When you walked through the front door, you were hit by a pleasant smell. The carpet was clean. The books on the shelves were arranged. A piano sat on one side of the sitting room. The lighting makes the place look very nice.

I remember waiting for one of these friends one evening. Instead of waiting downstairs as usual, his mother invited me to join them for dinner. After we ate, she did not allow the house maid to wash our plates. We took them to the kitchen ourselves.

My friend took a long time washing his single plate. I watched him, irritated. He was turning it, inspecting it, making sure every surface was clean. I thought he was being ridiculous. I washed mine in a few seconds and put it down.

The maid inspected both plates. Mine was not clean. She reported it to his mother.

I later heard that his mother made a comment about me. She said I was not trained.

I was angry. But later in life, I came to agree with her completely. In my house, we left all the dishes for the house maids. The concept of washing a plate until it was genuinely clean not just washed but inspected had never entered my thinking. I did not know what I did not know.

### The Cost of Invisible Gaps

The most dangerous detail is the one you cannot see, not because it is hidden, but because you have never been taught to look for it.

I have watched people walk into job interviews without having read their own CV. They are caught off guard by questions about their own experience. I have seen visa applications rejected at embassies because someone answered a straightforward question with something entirely unrelated. Not from dishonesty, but from a failure to listen carefully to what was actually being asked.

These are not failures of intelligence. They are failures of attention.

Look at how a skilled designer constructs a garment, a shoe, or a bag. The stitching. The alignment of the seam. The weight of the material. None of those decisions are accidental. Quality assurance exists precisely because someone understood that the details invisible to the casual eye are the ones that determine whether a product lasts or falls apart.

The customer who walks into a restaurant notices within minutes whether the tables have been properly cleaned, whether water arrived without being asked for, whether the toilets are maintained. They notice whether someone checked on them, or whether they had to chase a server for something that was promised and never came. They notice when no one asks how their visit was. None of these are grand gestures, they are small, repeatable disciplines. And their absence communicates one thing clearly: nobody here is paying attention.

Your thinking filters into everything you create. If your mind is disordered, your work will be disordered. If your standard for "clean" is "wiped quickly," that standard will show up in your products, your service, your contracts, your emails, and your relationships.


## 3. My Advice to the Next Generation

### Order is Where Attention Begins

You cannot develop an eye for detail inside a disordered environment. The first step is to create order around you, in your room, your desk, your workspace, your home. This is not about aesthetics. It is about training your brain to notice when something is not right.

When disorder is your baseline, everything looks acceptable. When order is your baseline, imperfection becomes immediately visible. You must raise your baseline.

Start with the space you work in every day. Make it clean. Make it deliberate. Then hold it to that standard. Every time you let disorder creep back in, you are training your attention to switch off. Every time you restore order, you are training it to stay on.

### Train Your Eyes

Surround yourself with quality. You cannot learn to recognise excellence if everything around you is counterfeit or careless. Study the details of well made things, the precision of a well designed product, the clarity of a well written document, the structure of a well run service. Ask yourself: *what specifically makes this good?* Name it. The more you can name what excellence looks like, the more quickly you will spot its absence.

When you produce work, anything, inspect it before you present it. Read it again. Look at it from the perspective of the person receiving it. Ask: would I be satisfied if someone handed this to me?

The plate is either clean or it is not. Train your eyes to tell the difference.

### Train Your Ears

What people say and what they mean are not always the same thing. Paying attention to detail in conversation means listening to understand, not just listening to respond.

I witnessed a sharp example of this at an airport. Two people got into an argument because one of them had jumped the queue. Eventually, the person who jumped the queue backed down and went to the back of the line. The practical problem was resolved.

But the argument continued.

The man who had jumped the queue was confused. He kept saying: *"I have gone back to the queue, why is he still shouting at me?"* He genuinely could not understand why the other man was still upset. As far as he was concerned, the issue was finished.

He was not listening.

The man was not shouting about the queue. The queue was done. He was saying something entirely different: *"You do not respect me."* That was the wound, and it had not been acknowledged, let alone addressed.

Two conversations were happening simultaneously. One man was talking about a physical position in a line. The other was talking about dignity. They were not speaking the same language, and the man who had caused the offence could not hear the difference.

This happens constantly in airports, in offices, in marriages, in meetings. Someone resolves the surface problem and walks away confused about why the other person is still upset. The surface problem was never really the problem. The detail they missed was the feeling underneath it.

When someone asks you a question, pause before answering. Make sure you are answering what was actually asked, not the question you expected, not the question you would have preferred. Many interviews are lost, many relationships damaged, and many instructions misunderstood because someone heard the surface of a question and missed its intent.

Listen for what is not being said as much as what is. The customer who says "everything was fine" without smiling is telling you something. The colleague who says "no problem" while looking away is telling you something. The detail you miss in a conversation can cost as much as the detail you miss in a product.

### Hold Yourself to a Finishing Standard

It is not enough to do a thing. You must do it to completion, and completion means the standard you would be proud to have inspected.

Before you submit anything, a document, a piece of work, an email, an application, check it. Read your own CV before you walk into an interview. Reread your email before you send it. Inspect your work before you hand it over. These are not extra steps. They are the difference between the person who is trusted with more and the person who is not.

The small things you overlook tell people everything about how you handle the big ones.

### Build a Culture of Detail Around You

If you run a team, a business, or a household, the standard of attention you model is the standard your environment will reflect back at you.

A restaurant where the tables are not cleaned, where orders are forgotten, where no one asks how the meal was. That restaurant is run by someone who has stopped paying attention. The gap between a good business and a great one is almost always found in the details that nobody bothered to fix because nobody was watching closely enough.

Watch closely. Ask the question. Check the table. Follow up on the thing that was promised. Build the habit of completion into everything you do, and everyone around you will begin to operate at a higher standard, often without being told.
