# Chapter 11: Dealing With People

## 1. The Lesson I Was Forced to Learn

One of the greatest challenges you will face in life is not the market, not the competition, not the economy. It is other human beings.

People are spectacularly creative at causing problems. Whether deliberately or completely by accident, human beings have an extraordinary ability to complicate the simplest situations, to misread the clearest intentions, and to introduce friction into arrangements that should be straightforward. If I were to write down everything I have personally experienced through people alone, it would fill at least three books.

But I want to give you the primary patterns the ways people most reliably cause damage so that you can recognise them early and respond wisely rather than react emotionally.


## The Primary Ways People Cause Friction

**Communication Breakdowns:** Most conflict does not begin with malice it begins with assumption. People assume they have been understood. They assume others know what they mean. They drop hints instead of speaking plainly, use sarcasm instead of honesty, and give the silent treatment instead of having a direct conversation. Meanwhile, information passed from person to person mutates a minor detail becomes a scandal, a small misunderstanding becomes a grievance. By the time a message reaches its fifth carrier, it often bears no resemblance to what was originally said.

**Ego and Power Struggles:** Some people change their expectations mid-game so that you can never actually satisfy them. The target moves every time you get close. Others take the applause when things go well and distribute blame when they do not. Some will actively work to undermine your success, not because it threatens them practically, but because it highlights their own stagnation.

**Emotional Drain and Manipulation:** There are people who use others as emotional dumping grounds, taking constantly, offering nothing in return. There are people who occupy the role of permanent victim, using it to make others feel guilty and obligated. There are people who will make you question your own memory, your own perception, your own sanity. Not because you are wrong, but because your accuracy is inconvenient to their narrative.

**Boundary Violations:** Some people operate with the quiet assumption that their time, their feelings, and their needs are inherently more important than yours. They will attempt to solve problems you did not ask them to solve, often creating larger ones in the process. They will ignore explicit boundaries, emotional, professional, or physical. Because they have decided the rules do not apply to them.

**Reliability and Accountability Deficits:** Some people make promises they have no intention of keeping, leaving you to manage the consequences of their commitment. Some deliberately perform tasks poorly so they are never asked to do them again. And the most dangerous combination is someone who breaks a promise and then convinces you it was your fault they forgot.


## 2. The Stories of My Journey

### The Farmer and the Land

There was a time I purchased land in Lagos, Nigeria. My agent told me the owner needed to raise funds urgently before travelling abroad, and the land was well positioned. I needed to build an office, so I moved quickly. I had two weeks before returning to London and wanted as much progress as possible before I left.

When I arrived at the land, I found a farmer using part of it to grow crops. He approached me and asked if I could give him a few months to harvest before we began construction. I could not wait, but out of goodwill, I asked him how much his harvest was worth. He gave me a figure I knew was inflated. I paid it anyway. He was grateful.

I then needed labourers to clear the land and remove the tree stumps. The farmer offered to take the job. I gave him half the payment upfront, made clear the work was urgent, and told him construction was starting the following day.

The next day I returned after midday. Nothing had been done. A neighbour went to the farmer's house down the road to tell him I had arrived. When he eventually appeared, he told me he had been sleeping, because it had been raining. The day before, he had told me the weather was too hot to work. He had a reason for every hour he had not moved.

I brought in specialist labourers to complete the work.

I later discovered the truth. The farmer had taken the advance payment and gone out that night. He had drunk too much and could not get out of bed. The money I gave him in good faith went into one evening of celebration. The work I needed urgently was simply never on his agenda.

Meanwhile, something happened to my agent separately. He had been trying to collect his commission from the land seller, who kept delaying. Eventually the seller travelled abroad without paying. Within a few months, the man was deported from the country he had travelled to.

I do not take satisfaction in that outcome. But it is a reminder that how people treat others eventually catches up with them.

In my case, the damage was manageable. It cost me time and patience but nothing irreplaceable. That is not always the story. Be careful who you bring onto your projects and into your commitments. The wrong person can cause problems that take years to untangle.

### The Tax Bill and the Friends Who Were Not There

There was a period when I faced a tax issue in the United Kingdom. The resolution was simple in principle I needed to transfer funds from my personal account to my business account before a deadline, which would have resolved the liability. My accountant gave me the instruction with less than twenty-four hours to spare. There was no time to arrange the funds myself.

I reached out to several close friends. People I had known for years. People I knew had the funds sitting idle in accounts, doing nothing. I was not asking for a gift. I was asking to borrow money for a matter of days. Not a single one was willing to help.

I was gutted. Not primarily by the financial consequence, though that was significant, but by the revelation.

The deadline passed. The tax authorities issued me a bill of over £25,000. I had assets I could have sold to clear it immediately, but I chose not to. I made a deliberate decision to sit with the pain of a monthly repayment plan with interest accruing for the three years it took to clear the debt. I wanted the experience to be permanently pressed into my memory. I wanted to know, without any softening, what it felt like to discover the real limits of people you had trusted.

These were not acquaintances. These were friends I had done significant favours for in the past. The experience did not make me bitter. But it made me clear.

A friend is born for adversity. That is a proverb, not a sentiment. And it is true. The people who are with you in good times are not always the people who will stand with you when things are difficult. The people who smile and say thank you do not always respect what the resource you gave them actually cost you to obtain.


## 3. My Advice to the Next Generation

### When You Have Money, It Is Difficult to Know Who Loves You

Wealth attracts proximity. People will attach themselves to your resources, your access, your network, not always with bad intentions, but not always with your interests at heart either. When things are going well, your circle grows. When things get hard, it often reveals itself to be much smaller than you thought.

This is not a reason to be suspicious of everyone. It is a reason to observe people over time before extending your deepest trust.

### Test Your Inner Circle

The people closest to you should be tested, not maliciously, but deliberately. Give small requests. Observe how people respond when you are not at your best. Watch what they do when you are not in the room. Over time, the people who consistently show up, who protect your interests when it would be easier not to, who tell you the truth when you would prefer a comfortable lie those people identify themselves. Hold them tightly.

### Prepare for Betrayal Before It Happens

People will let you down. This is not pessimism, it is preparation. The question to ask yourself about anyone you trust with something important is: *what will I do if this person betrays me?* Not if when. Having a plan in advance means you will not be destroyed by what is almost inevitable across a long enough life. Preparation does not prevent betrayal. It determines how much damage it causes.

### Not Everyone Who Smiles Respects What You Gave

Investing in people is a good and worthy thing to do. But be honest with yourself about what you are doing. Not everyone who says thank you understands what the resource you gave them actually cost you. Not everyone who benefits from your sacrifice will honour it. Give generously, but do not confuse gratitude with loyalty, and do not confuse politeness with depth.

### Always Define Your Exit Strategy Before You Commit

If you make a commitment to support someone, financially, professionally, emotionally, be explicit from the very beginning about where that commitment ends. If you agree to pay someone's school fees, state clearly upfront for how many years. If you are providing ongoing support of any kind, name the conditions and the limits before they become assumed.

This is not hardness. It is wisdom. The person who is grateful for your support today may become resentful of its removal tomorrow, if the removal was never part of the conversation. An unspoken expectation is a future grievance waiting to happen. Define your terms early, kindly, and clearly, and both sides will be protected.

### Ask Where Their Interest Sits

When someone gives you advice, counsel, a warning, or a recommendation, always ask yourself one question before you act on it: *where does their interest sit?*

If the person advising you benefits from your following that advice, scrutinise it carefully. This does not mean they are wrong, but it means their perspective is not neutral, and you should account for that. The doctor whose income depends on a particular diagnosis, the financial adviser who earns commission on a product, the friend who benefits from a decision you are about to make none of these people are necessarily dishonest. But their interest shapes what they see, and what they share.

Think for yourself. Weigh the advice against the adviser's position. Then decide.

### It Is an Illusion to Think Everyone Likes You

Not everyone wishes you well. This is not a comfortable truth, but it is a necessary one. Some people will support you publicly and undermine you privately. Some will celebrate your face and envy your progress. Understanding this does not mean living in suspicion, it means living with clarity. You do not need everyone to like you. You need to know who actually does.

### Guard the Rare Ones

If you find people who will genuinely stand with you through difficulty, who are present when you are struggling, who do not disappear when your resources run low, who protect your name in rooms you are not in, hold them close. They are not common. They are a treasure. Honour them, invest in them, and never take for granted what it means to have people like that in your life.
