# Chapter 10: The Numbers Game

## 1. The Lesson I Was Forced to Learn

After following all the advice in this book, building your purpose, protecting your vision, managing your energy, doing the work, paying attention to detail, running your own race, managing your time. There will still be moments when nothing seems to be working. When the results are not coming. When you begin to wonder whether you are doing something wrong, or whether the whole thing was a mistake.

In those moments, I want you to understand something that took me years to grasp: life is a numbers game.

The question is not whether you will succeed. The question is how many attempts it will take before you do. And the answer to that question is almost always: more than you have tried so far.

Most people stop before the number. They knock on ten doors, hear nothing back, and conclude the door cannot be opened. What they have actually discovered is that their number is eleven. Or twenty. Or a hundred. The people who succeed are not always the most talented. They are often simply the ones who kept going long enough to find the number that worked and who used every failed attempt to improve the next one.


## 2. The Stories of My Journey

### Ten Years in the Comfort Zone

In the year 2000, I started working for a multinational company in the United Kingdom. I became their IT director. The role came with everything stability, benefits, status, a comfortable salary, and the quiet assurance that tomorrow would look much like today.

I stayed for over ten years.

In those ten years, I was not building. I was sitting. The job was good, and because it was good, I stopped pushing. I stopped testing myself. I stopped playing the numbers game. I had found a comfortable place inside a system someone else had built, and I settled into it so thoroughly that I began to rot slowly, imperceptibly, in the way that things rot when they stop moving.

Then the company lost a major contract. I was laid off.

That redundancy was one of the most uncomfortable things that had ever happened to me professionally. But it was also the most clarifying. The comfort I had been living in was not stability, it was stagnation wearing the mask of security. I had not been growing. I had been drifting. And when the external structure that had been holding me up disappeared, I discovered how much of my capability I had quietly allowed to silently decay.

From that point, I made a decision. I moved into the contracting world, where I would be responsible for myself entirely. No safety net. No permanent position. Every contract I signed was typically six months, renewable only if I was delivering. I could be let go at any moment with no strings attached.

That pressure was the best thing that could have happened to me.

Because I was always close to the door, I had to stay at the cutting edge of my industry. Because I was always in the market, I got good at being in the market. I attended frequent contract interviews and became skilled at winning them. I worked across different projects and my experience compounded rapidly. I learned the process how long it takes, what works, what does not, how to improve my odds with each attempt.

When I first started contracting, I typically had to apply for more than twenty contracts before winning one. Over time, through deliberate refinement of what I was offering and how I presented it, I brought that number down to approximately five. Not because the market became easier, because I became better. I had built credibility, a reputation, and a clear understanding of what my clients needed. I had mastered the numbers game by playing it consistently.

Contracting also gave me something that a permanent role never could: freedom. I can choose when I work and when I step back to focus on building my own businesses. That flexibility is not available to someone locked into a permanent position waiting for annual leave. It is one of the most underrated advantages of the contracting world. You are not just selling your skills, you are buying back control of your time.


## 3. My Advice to the Next Generation

### Understand Your Conversion Rate

If you send out a hundred CVs and receive one interview, you now have data. To get two interviews, you need to send approximately two hundred. If you win one job from two interviews, after sending out two hundred applications. To win two jobs, you need about three hundred applications and three interviews.

This is not discouraging. It is empowering. It means the outcome is not random, it is mathematical. Once you know your conversion rate, you know exactly what is required to achieve the result you want.

When people come to me saying they cannot find work, my first question is always: how many applications have you sent? If the answer is fifty with no interviews, there are only two possibilities either the CV needs improving, or the volume is not yet high enough to generate traction. Either way, the solution is clear and actionable.

The same principle applies to winning business, sourcing clients, building an audience, or pursuing any goal that requires repeated attempts. Most people give up before they have knocked enough doors. They have not failed they have simply not yet reached their number.

### Improve Across Every Attempt

Playing the numbers game is not about mindless repetition. It is about intelligent iteration. Every attempt that does not work is data. Every door that does not open tells you something about either the door or the knock.

Ask yourself after every rejection: what can I improve? Was it the CV? The way I presented myself in the interview? The type of opportunity I was targeting? Was the product or service genuinely solving a problem people are willing to pay for?

If people can get what you are offering for free, they will not pay for it. If the problem you are solving is not one they feel urgently, they will not exchange money for your solution. The market is the most honest feedback mechanism available to you. When the numbers are not working, the market is telling you something. Listen to it, adjust, and go again.

### Learn to Sell

Selling is not a dirty word. It is one of the most essential skills you will ever develop, and almost every significant outcome in life requires it. Whether you are selling a product, a service, yourself in an interview, or an idea to someone you need to persuade.

What you present to the world your CV, your pitch, your work, your reputation is a product. That product reflects who you are. Make sure it is honest. Make sure it has integrity. Make sure it solves a real problem that real people care about. And then learn how to communicate its value clearly.

The person who has a great product but cannot communicate its value will always lose to the person with a good product who can. Learn to sell.

### Movement Creates Momentum

There is a law in this universe: anything that stays in one place slowly dies. This applies to water. It applies to muscles. It applies to careers.

The best way to increase your earning is to move. The longer you stay in the same position, the less incentive a company has to pay you more. Organisations will frequently hire a new person and pay them significantly more than they would ever give you as an existing employee. The market rewards movement it rewards the person who is visible, testing themselves, and demonstrating current value.

When you are in a permanent role, make it a regular discipline to test the market. Apply for other opportunities. Attend interviews. Know your value in the current market. You may choose to stay where you are, that is a legitimate decision, but you should stay by choice, with current information, not by default.

Do not wait for someone to promote you. The bread does not come to those who sit still. You have to go out and fight for it.

### Find the Courage to Keep Going

Success does not visit the settled. It finds the people who are still in motion when it arrives.

In the beginning, the numbers will feel discouraging. Twenty applications. Thirty. Fifty. No response. I know what that feels like. I have lived it. But I also know what is on the other side of it because I kept going until I found it.

The courage to keep going is not a feeling. It is a decision you make before the feeling arrives. You decide in advance that you will not stop at the first rejection, or the tenth, or the twentieth. You decide that every attempt is an investment in the version of you who eventually wins.

Start. Adjust. Keep going. The number is closer than you think.
