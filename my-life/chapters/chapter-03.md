# Chapter 3: Knowing When to Talk

When you finally land on a clear, exciting vision — whether it's a new business, a creative project, or a major life change — the natural instinct is to broadcast it. You want others to feel the same enthusiasm you do.

However, being highly selective about who gets to hear your vision is one of the most effective ways to protect it. Here is why broadcasting your goals prematurely can actually work against you.


## 1. The "Premature Accomplishment" Trap

Psychology research, most notably by Peter Gollwitzer at NYU, shows that sharing your goals can actually make you less likely to achieve them.

When you share your vision and someone responds with, "That's amazing! You're going to do great," your brain releases dopamine. You receive the social reward of the goal before you've done any of the actual work. This tricks your mind into feeling a premature sense of accomplishment, which significantly drops your motivation to execute the hard, unglamorous steps required to get there.


## 2. Early Ideas are Fragile

A new vision is like a seedling: it has potential, but it can't survive harsh weather yet.

**The Naysayers:** Some people will project their own risk aversion onto you, pointing out all the reasons it will fail.

**The Pragmatists:** Even well-meaning friends might demand concrete logistics ("How exactly will you fund this?") before you've had time to figure them out, causing you to panic and abandon the idea.

Until your vision has a strong enough foundation to withstand critique, sharing it exposes it to premature judgment.


## 3. The Motivation Shift

When a vision is private, your motivation is intrinsic — you are doing it for yourself because you believe in it.

The moment you tell everyone, you introduce external pressure. Now, your motivation can easily shift to extrinsic factors: you start working to avoid looking foolish, to prove the doubters wrong, or to meet the timeline you bragged about. This kind of anxiety-driven motivation leads to burnout much faster than passion does.


## 4. The Energy Leak

Talking about doing something feels remarkably similar to actually doing it. If you spend hours explaining your vision, defending it, and hyping it up to friends, you are draining the creative and mental energy that should be applied to building the project itself.


## The Power of the Tongue

The ancient wisdom that words have the power to create or destroy adds a profound layer to why you must guard your vision. Whether you view this psychologically or spiritually, words do not just describe reality — they actively shape it.

When you introduce the power of the tongue into the equation, broadcasting your vision becomes not just a matter of lost motivation, but of protecting the integrity and life force of the idea itself.

### Inviting "Spoken Death" Over Your Seed

An old proverb in the Bible states, *"Death and life are in the power of the tongue."* When a vision is new, it is fragile. By sharing it recklessly, you invite others to speak their own fears, limitations, and cynicism over it.

**The Echo Effect:** Even if you brush off a friend's skeptical comment in the moment, their words can echo in your mind later. When you inevitably hit a roadblock, their spoken doubt (*"I told you that was too risky"*) can rise to the surface and become your own internal monologue.

**Projected Limitations:** Most people cannot see what you see. When they speak negatively about your vision, they are usually verbalizing their own boundaries, not yours. But once those words are spoken over your project, they introduce friction that wasn't there before.

### Diluting Your Own Decree

Your words carry creative weight. When you declare a vision out loud, it is a decree — a binding commitment that sets action into motion.

If you use your tongue to casually chatter about your goals to anyone who will listen, you cheapen the currency of your own word. You train your subconscious to treat your declarations as mere conversational entertainment rather than promises. Keeping your mouth shut preserves the potency of your intention; when you finally do speak about it, it is backed by undeniable action.

### The Energy Leak (Revisited Through the Tongue)

Words require energy. Explaining, defending, and hyping up a vision consumes a massive amount of mental and creative force. Every hour spent talking about what you are going to do is an hour of creative energy diverted away from the project itself.


## Seeking Those Who "Speak Life"

Because the tongue is so powerful, the circle of people you *do* tell becomes even more important. You should only expose your vision to people whose words will act as water, not poison.

Look for people who practice the power of the tongue positively:

- **The Builders:** People who respond to your vision by speaking actionable strategy and encouragement over it.
- **The Guardrails:** Mentors who don't just flatter you, but use their words to warn you of blind spots while affirming your capability.
- **The Believers:** Those who have a track record of seeing the potential in things before they exist, and who will remind you of your own vision when you start to doubt it.


## Who Should You Tell?

Keeping a vision quiet doesn't mean operating in total isolation. You just need to shift from broadcasting to strategic sharing. Only share your vision with three types of people:

- **Collaborators:** People who are actively building it with you.
- **Mentors:** People who have accomplished something similar and can offer actionable, experienced guidance.
- **Accountability Partners:** Select one or two people who won't just praise you, but will actually ask you, *"Did you finish that step you promised to do this week?"*

Protect your vision until it is strong enough to speak for itself.


## How to Handle Negative Comments

It can feel like a punch to the gut when you share something close to your heart and someone dumps cold water on it. Our brains are wired with a negativity bias — meaning we register negative feedback much more intensely than positive feedback. Their words can stick in your head like lint, playing on a loop.

To shake off their words and reclaim your confidence, you need to actively change how you process what they said. Here is a strategy to neutralize the negativity and get your momentum back.

### 1. Trace the Source (Consider the Messenger)

When someone speaks negatively about your vision, it is rarely a reflection of your idea's viability. Instead, it is a reflection of their limits. Ask yourself:

- Have they built what you are trying to build? If someone who has never started a business tells you your business idea won't work, their feedback is unqualified.
- Are they projecting fear? Often, when people love us, they want to protect us from failure. Their negativity is just fear wearing the mask of "practicality."
- Are they comfortable with their own stagnation? Sometimes, when you announce you are leveling up, it acts as a mirror to other people's lack of progress. Their criticism is an attempt to pull you back down so they don't feel left behind.

**The Reality Check:** You wouldn't take financial advice from someone who is broke, so do not take vision advice from someone who isn't building anything.

### 2. Separate "Noise" From "Signal"

Is there a tiny grain of truth in what they said that could actually make your vision stronger?

If their comment was pure cynicism (*"That's stupid, it'll never work"*), that is noise. Flush it out completely.

But if their comment was harsh but pointed to a real logistical gap (*"How will you handle inventory?"*), that is a signal. Take the signal, throw away the negative emotional wrapper it came in, and use it as a prompt to tighten your strategy. By turning their criticism into a problem-solving exercise, you take back control.

### 3. Rewrite the Script (The Pivot)

Every time their negative comment pops into your head, do not fight it — replace it. If you try to force yourself not to think about it, it will persist. Instead, use a pivot phrase:

| When the memory plays... | Instantly counter with... |
|---|---|
| "They said I don't have enough experience." | "I will gain the experience by building the thing." |
| "They said the market is too crowded." | "The market is crowded because there's high demand. I bring a unique angle." |
| "They said it's a pipe dream." | "They can't see the picture because they aren't the ones holding the paintbrush." |

### 4. Go Underground (The Silence Remedy)

The best way to regain your confidence is through action, not argument. Do not try to win them over, do not explain yourself further, and do not try to prove them wrong with words.

Go completely quiet about the project around them. Take all that defensive energy and channel it directly into execution. Let your progress be the only statement you make. There is an incredible, quiet confidence that comes from moving the needle forward on your project while the naysayers think you've given up on it.

### 5. Cleanse the Subconscious

Your mind needs a palate cleanser. Intentionally spend the next 48 hours consuming content that fuels your vision. Read biographies of people who built impossible things, listen to podcasts in your niche, or talk to that one friend who always leaves you feeling energized. Flood the engine with clean fuel so the toxic feedback gets crowded out.

Your vision wasn't given to them — it was given to you. They literally cannot see it the way you do, and they don't need to. Pack up your tools, close the door to the critics, and get back to work.


## The Death of a Vision

When I was fourteen, the world felt vastly large and entirely open to interpretation. Around that time, a young man came to live with us as our house help, taking on the brunt of the family's daily chores. Despite the difference in our roles, a genuine friendship sparked between us. We weren't just two teenagers sharing a roof; we became partners in imagination.

At the back of our house lay a wild, forgotten patch of land. Overgrown with untamed bush and choked by weeds, it belonged to no one we knew. To the rest of the neighborhood, it was an eyesore. To us, it was a blank canvas.

One afternoon, standing at the edge of the overgrowth, a vision gripped us: We were going to build a farm.

The excitement was intoxicating. We pooled together every single penny of our meager savings, sacrificing everything we had to buy the basic tools we would need. The day we set out to clear the land, the sun was unforgiving. It was grueling, back-breaking labor. Sweat stung our eyes and our muscles ached, but we didn't care. With every swing of the cutlass and every cleared vine, we were carving out our future. We were almost done. The finish line was in sight.

Then, the neighbor from next door appeared.

Leaning over the boundary, she called out, "What are you guys doing?"

Wiping the sweat from our brows, we looked up at her with beaming faces. We poured our hearts out. We told her about our vision, our plans to plant crops, and our dreams of selling the harvest. We expected encouragement, or at least curiosity.

Instead, she brought an icy wave of negativity.

"Why are you doing that?" she asked, her voice dripping with disapproval. "You shouldn't be doing that. Do you even know who the land belongs to? Who is the owner?"

We fell silent. "No," we admitted.

"Well, you shouldn't be doing it," she stated flatly. "It's wrong."

With just a few sharp, doubting words, the wind was completely knocked out of our sails. The heavy weight of self-doubt crashed down on us. Right then and there, our vision died.

But our neighbor wasn't entirely finished with us. She pointed toward a wild guava tree buried in the brush we hadn't yet touched. "Well, what you *can* do," she suggested, suddenly shifting her tone, "is just clear the bush around that tree so it can grow well."

Disheartened and lacking the strength to argue, we did what she asked.

It was only later that the bitter truth revealed itself. We discovered that she frequently sneaked into that bush to harvest the guavas for herself. She hadn't stopped us out of civic duty; she had manipulated our labor to clear a path for her own convenience. She shrunk our grand farm into her personal fruit basket.

Years passed. Decades, actually.

The mysterious landowner never showed up. No one ever stepped forward to claim that forgotten patch of earth. But eventually, the truth came to light in the most ironic way possible: the land had belonged to my father all along — he sold it to the chief justice of the town.

Today, if you walk out to the back of that property, you won't find a wild bush, and you won't find a couple of boys daydreaming in the sun. Today, a massive, thriving school stands proudly on that very soil.

That school is a monument to a painful, yet invaluable life lesson. Our instincts weren't wrong. The land was full of potential, and it was ours to build upon. Our only mistake was exposing a fragile, newborn dream to the harsh winds of outside opinion.

**The Lesson:** Be fiercely protective of your vision when it is young. When a dream is still in its infancy, it does not have the strength to survive skepticism, malice, or the hidden agendas of others. Build in silence, let your roots grow deep, and only share your harvest when the tree is strong enough to stand on its own.


## The Delayed Calling

In the 1990s, I was living in the United Kingdom. I attended a party one evening where I found myself chatting with two women. As the music and conversation flowed, one of the ladies began to share her heart. She was practically beaming with excitement as she told us about her dream: she wanted to become a nurse.

She had already taken the terrifying first step — she had filled out the application forms and was ready to submit them to the university. You could feel the electric energy of someone standing on the precipice of a new life.

But before her excitement could even settle in the room, the second lady cut her down.

"Nursing is a terrible idea," she asserted flatly. "There are absolutely no jobs out there. It's a dead-end profession. Nothing good is going to come from you going into nursing."

The disapproval was so heavy, so absolute, that it acted like a suffocating blanket on a fire. The first lady's confidence crumbled right before my eyes. Paralyzed by the fear of making a massive mistake, she quietly shelved her application. She surrendered her dream.

Nearly a decade passed.

One day, I made a startling discovery. The second lady — the very one who had adamantly declared that nursing was a dead-end, jobless disaster — had quietly gone to university and become a registered nurse herself. She hadn't given that advice out of a genuine, objective warning; she had looked at a good idea, convinced her friend to drop it, and then stolen the blueprint for herself.

When the first lady eventually found out about this betrayal, it was a massive wake-up call. Realizing she had been manipulated into abandoning her own destiny, she refused to let the dream die entirely. She dusted off her decade-old ambition, enrolled in university, and finally became a nurse.

Today, all these years later, I am still in touch with both of them. They are both successful nurses, now gracefully approaching the horizon of their retirement.

While this story eventually had a triumphant ending, it highlights a different, more insidious shade of human nature than the story of my childhood farm.

**The Lesson:** Sometimes, people don't criticize your vision because they think it's bad. They criticize it because they recognize how good it is, and they want to clear you out of the way so they can claim it for themselves. When someone tries to talk you out of your calling, look closely at their motives. Never let someone else's manufactured fear talk you out of a future that belongs to you.
