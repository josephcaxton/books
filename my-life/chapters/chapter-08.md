# Chapter 8: Run Your Own Race

## 1. The Lesson I Was Forced to Learn

It took me many years to learn a truth that cost me time, money, and momentum before I accepted it: I was not built for other people's races.

Anything involving competition, measuring myself against someone else's pace, chasing what is currently fashionable, jumping into whatever everyone around me seems to be doing, I have learned to leave alone. Every time I tried to run in someone else's race, I lost my footing. I lost my rhythm. I lost the quiet, consistent momentum that I had built up on my own path. And picking it back up again after that kind of derailment was always harder than it should have been.

We are wired differently. If competition sharpens you, if comparison motivates you, if trends give you direction, use those tools. They work for some people. But if your brain is wired like mine, chasing the crowd will not make you faster. It will make you lost.

The lesson I was forced to learn is this: there is a pace that belongs to you. When you are moving at it, things build. When you abandon it to follow a trend or match someone else's speed, things unravel. The noise of what everyone else is doing is not your direction. It is a distraction dressed up as an opportunity.


## 2. The Stories of My Journey

### The Nigerian Trend Machine

Over the years I have travelled back to Nigeria regularly, and I began to notice a pattern that told me something important about how human beings lose themselves to the crowd.

Each time I arrived, there was a new thing. Not a new idea, a new trend. And not just a few people exploring it. Almost everyone.

One trip, football was everywhere. Every parent I spoke to wanted their child to become a professional footballer. The game was on every television, in every conversation, in every compound. The next time I came back, it was pure water. Everywhere you turned, someone had set up a small pure water production operation, at the roadside, in a back room, in a small space beside their home. Then it was event centres. People were constructing event centres, convinced that owning one was the guaranteed path to financial security. If you had an event centre, you were made.

The trend always looked different. The logic was always the same: *this is what is working now, so this is what I should do.*

What I observed across all of these waves was that most of the people joining them had no particular knowledge of the business, no existing skill, no genuine calling toward it. They were moving because others were moving. They were building because others were building. And when the trend faded, as trends always do, many of them were left with investments that made no sense outside the moment that had created them.

This is not a Nigerian problem. It is a human one. As I write this, a significant number of people in the United Kingdom are trying to retrain into technology, not because they are drawn to it, not because they have discovered a genuine aptitude for it, but because they have been told it pays well. Some of them will thrive. Many will not, because no amount of money is enough motivation to sustain the deep work that a field requires from you when the novelty wears off.

Do not go where the crowd is going. Go where you are built to go.

### The Car Valeting Business

The first business I opened in the United Kingdom was a mobile car valeting business. It failed within six months.

The reason was simple: I knew nothing about car valeting. I was trained in technology. I understood IT. I had no knowledge of the valeting industry, not the work itself, not the standards, not the pricing, not how to manage staff in that environment.

My staff understood this before I did. They knew I had no way of measuring whether the work was being done properly, no way of verifying whether the money was coming in as it should. They did what people sometimes do when they are supervised by someone who does not know the business, they took advantage. Work was done but money was pocketed. I was paying wages for results I could not properly account for.

The business was not poorly located. It was not poorly timed. It failed because I had no business being in it. I had opened a business in someone else's lane using someone else's knowledge, and I paid for it.

### The Rich Man's Wife

I knew a woman whose husband was very wealthy. She had everything she needed materially. She stayed home, looked after their children, and lived comfortably. But after a while, she grew restless. She wanted her own income. Her own business. Her own thing.

Her husband resisted. He told her she had everything, why not simply be present for the children? But she pressed, and eventually he gave in. He gave her money to start whatever business she chose.

She opened a large, well stocked shop. But she knew nothing about selling. She had no relationship with her customers, no understanding of their behaviour or needs. What she did not know, what her wealth had shielded her from knowing was that the people who succeed in that kind of retail business typically do not begin by renting and stocking a shop. They begin on the street. They source customers themselves, bring them to the stores of others, watch how transactions happen, learn the rhythm of the market and the needs of the buyer from the ground up. By the time they open their own shop, they know who their customer is, what they want, how they decide, and when they walk away.

She assumed that the reason others started from the street was because they lacked the capital to do anything else. She had capital. She skipped the beginning.

But the beginning was not a financial problem. The beginning was the education. The street was the classroom. She bypassed it, she opened a large shop with no idea how to run it.

The business did not last a year.


## 3. My Advice to the Next Generation

### Run Your Own Race

There is a pace that belongs to you. You will feel it when you are in it. A quiet momentum, a sense of building, a forward movement that does not require you to constantly look sideways to check if you are keeping up. When you abandon that pace to chase what someone else is doing, you trade something real for something borrowed.

Stay on your path. When you see a trend, you are welcome to observe it. But before you act on it, ask yourself honestly: *do I have genuine knowledge of this? Am I drawn to this, or am I drawn to the reward others seem to be getting from it?* Those are very different things, and the answer will tell you everything you need to know.

### Know the Business Before You Open the Business

Every business requires a specific body of knowledge. Not general intelligence, specific knowledge. The knowledge of the customer. The knowledge of the product. The knowledge of the margins, the workflow, the likely problems, and how they are solved.

If you do not have that knowledge, the people around you who do will take advantage of the gap. Not always maliciously, sometimes simply because the gap exists and human nature fills it.

Do not open a business in a field you have not studied. Before you invest money, invest time. Work in the industry. Observe it from the inside. Learn what the successful people in it actually do, not what it looks like from the outside. The view from the street is very different from the view from inside the shop.

### The Beginning is the Education

If you find yourself thinking that the only reason other people started at the bottom was because they had no choice, that if you had capital you could skip that stage, you are making an expensive mistake.

The bottom is not a financial position. It is an educational one. The person who starts from nothing and works their way up is not doing so because they are poor. They are doing so because that path teaches them things that cannot be learned any other way. They learn the customer. They learn the market. They learn what works and what fails before they have put everything on the line.

Capital without knowledge is not an advantage. It is a faster way to lose more.

Do not let pride or resources talk you out of learning from the beginning. The beginning is where the real education happens.

### Do Not Follow the Noise

Trends are not callings. Fashionable businesses are not the same as good businesses for you. The wave that everyone is riding right now will eventually break and when it does, the people who built on the wave will have nothing left, while the people who built on their genuine purpose will still be standing.

Every generation has its version of the football craze, the pure water business, the event centre, the technology retraining rush. The noise is always loud. The noise always sounds like opportunity.

Your calling is quieter than the noise. It was there before the trend arrived, and it will still be there after the trend has passed. The question is not what everyone else is doing. The question is what you were built to do and whether you are doing it.
