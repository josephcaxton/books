# Chapter 6: Be the Best You Can Be

## 1. The Lesson I Was Forced to Learn

There is a difference between the person who wins the contract and the person who came second. One gets paid. The other goes home.

In many areas of life, second place is a respectable achievement. But in a competitive field where only one person is needed, second best is functionally the same as last. It does not matter how good you are if someone else is better. That is not harsh — that is simply the reality of how the world works. And once you accept it, you stop being offended by it and start preparing for it.

What I was forced to learn over many years of building a career in technology is that becoming the best you can be is not a goal you reach once and tick off. It is a permanent assignment. The moment you stop learning, someone else overtakes you — quietly, without announcement — and one day you discover that the world has moved on without you.


## 2. The Stories of My Journey

### The Notes Bank

Over the course of more than twenty years, I have built something I consider one of my most valuable professional assets. Not a degree. Not a certification. A notes bank.

It started simply enough. After every job interview I attended — whether I won the contract or not — I would sit down as soon as I left and write out every question I had been asked. Then I would go away and research every area where I had stumbled, every gap the interviewers had exposed. I would add what I learned to my notes, turn the knowledge into questions and answers, and file it away.

I use the Notes app on my Apple Mac. It syncs with my iPhone. On the train, at the bus stop, in a waiting room, in any spare moment — I open my phone and review the questions and answers I have accumulated. I revise them. I update them. I treat them the way a professional athlete treats match footage: not to dwell on past performances, but to close the gap between where I am and where I need to be.

Before walking into a contract interview, I study the job specification carefully, then revise my notes against it. I arrive having already answered the likely questions in my own mind. I have won contracts that others did not because of the information sitting in that note bank. Twenty years of feedback, research, and deliberate refinement, all available on a phone in my pocket.

That system did not build itself. It was built one interview at a time, one piece of feedback at a time.

### The Designer Who Never Got Her Foot in the Door

Years ago, I knew a family member whose daughter had just finished secondary school. She was talented — genuinely gifted in design. Her parents' instinct was to push her toward A-levels and then university. The conventional path.

At the time, I was on a contract building an iPhone app for a client based near Bond Street in London. If you know London, you know that some of the best design agencies in the country are clustered around that area — Bond Street, Oxford Street — working for global brands, producing work that gets seen everywhere. I was inside that world. I had access.

I told the parents: don't spend the money on university yet. Let me introduce her to some of the designers at these agencies. Let her get her foot in the door. Let her offer to work for free, even if it means making tea, running errands, sitting in the corner and watching how world class designers think and work. All they would need to cover was her daily travel fare.

They refused.

Their daughter was intelligent, they said. She could not be making tea for free. There was no point arguing. They could not see the value in what I was proposing, so she went to university instead.

She finished her degree. Then she spent years struggling to find work in her field — graduating into a competitive market with a qualification but no contacts, no portfolio built inside a real agency, and a student loan that would follow her for years.

Here is what would have happened if they had said yes. In the time she spent sitting in lectures, she could have been sitting next to some of the best designers in the UK, watching how they solve real problems for real clients. Internal staff always get first consideration before a company hires from outside. She would have built relationships. She would have built a portfolio. She would have become a known, trusted presence in one of the most competitive creative environments in the country. By the time her peers were graduating, she could have been one of the rising talents in the industry.

Pride cost her years.

The market does not care about certificates on paper. It cares about value. If I need someone to fix a pipe in my house, I am not asking to see their academic credentials — I am asking whether they can fix the pipe. Businesses are looking for people who can solve their problems. The person who can demonstrate that they solve problems gets the work. It is as simple as that.

### The Wedding Photographer Who Lost Everything

About a decade ago, a couple invited me to their wedding in Tottenham, London. A few weeks before the day, I had bought a professional camera from a shop in central London. I wanted to try my hand at photography — to find out if it was something I could pursue as a hobby. The wedding seemed like a perfect opportunity to give the camera a proper test.

On the day, I found myself alongside the official photographer. I noticed straight away that his camera was an older model. When he took a shot, his camera clicked once. Mine clicked about twenty times in a single burst. I had also bought a powerful lens to go with it. One press of my shutter and I had twenty frames — some sharp, some not, but the best ones were very good.

I decided to give the couple a wedding present. I used Apple's photo book service to put together a printed album from the best shots I had taken. When they received it, they were overjoyed.

Then they told me what had happened with the official photographer. He had lost all of their photographs. A technical failure — the images were simply gone. Every professional shot from their wedding day, wiped out.

If I had not been there with my camera, that couple would have had nothing. No record of one of the most important days of their lives.

The official photographer was hired and paid to do a job. He came with the wrong equipment. He had no backup. He was not operating at the standard the moment required — and his clients paid the price.

Be professional. Use the right tool for the right job. When someone trusts you with something important, your preparation is not just about you.

### The Air-Conditioning Engineer Worth Waiting For

For years, getting my air-conditioning units properly serviced was a frustrating, expensive cycle. There were plenty of engineers available, competing for work, offering their services, but the quality was deeply inconsistent. Some would service a unit and it would break down again within a month. Others would come in to fix a simple problem and leave behind a larger one. I began to wonder whether some of them were deliberately creating faults to guarantee repeat business, while others were simply not skilled enough to do the job properly.

Then someone introduced me to an engineer who worked for the University of Lagos. He was the sole engineer responsible for the university's entire air-conditioning infrastructure. He was not cheap. Compared to the local engineers I had been using, his rates were noticeably higher.

I gave him a try. And our air-conditioning problems, which had been a persistent source of frustration for years, quietly disappeared.

From that point, he became the only person I would allow to touch my units — at home and in my car.

There was one occasion when I was preparing to travel to Nigeria and one of the main sitting room units was underperforming. I called him, but he was on a project in the south east of the country and could not make it to Lagos in time. Against my better judgment, I tried a local engineer again.

He damaged it.

That was the last time I made that mistake. Now, I wait. However long it takes for the right engineer to become available, I wait. The cost of impatience is always higher than the cost of patience.

When you are the best at what you do, people will wait for you. They will pay your price. They will come back every time — not because they have to, but because the alternative is too costly. That is the quiet power of genuine excellence. It commands premium rates and it earns loyalty that no discount can buy.


### What the Feedback Taught Me

Asking for feedback after every interview was one of the most uncomfortable disciplines I ever adopted. Not many people do it. Most prefer to walk away quickly and protect their ego from the details of why they fell short.

Over the years, the feedback I received was sometimes blunt, sometimes specific, and occasionally confusing. Here is some of what I was told:

- *"We got someone with more experience than you."*
- *"You remained focused on executing day-to-day tasks instead of discussing high level business strategy."*
- *"You are saying all the right words, but we couldn't get a true sense of who you actually are as a teammate."*
- *"You have knowledge gaps in AWS DevOps engineering."*
- *"You did absolutely nothing wrong; another candidate simply possessed a singular, highly specific advantage."*
- *"The way you answered the questions is not structured, so we could not understand you."*

Every one of those comments went straight into my notes. Every one of them became a target for improvement. The feedback that stung the most was always the most useful — because it pointed to a real gap, not a hypothetical one.

The person who asks for feedback after failing is already ahead of the person who does not ask. And the person who acts on that feedback is ahead of both.


## 3. My Advice to the Next Generation

### Learning Never Stops

One of the most dangerous mistakes you can make is to confuse education with learning. They are not the same thing.

A university prepares you with the basics — the foundation. It gives you a structure to stand on. But true learning begins the moment you start doing. Every problem you solve, every mistake you make, every role you step into teaches you something that no classroom ever could. The people who think education ends at graduation stop growing the moment the world needs them to grow the most.

Commit to learning every day for the rest of your life. It is not optional if you want to be the best.

### Value Over Credentials

The market does not pay for what you know on paper. It pays for what you can do in practice.

This does not mean education is worthless — it means education is a starting point, not a finishing line. A degree gives you a foundation. Experience builds the house. The person who combines both will always outperform the person who has only one.

If you have an opportunity to get into a room with the best people in your field — even unpaid, even in a junior capacity, even making tea — take it. What you absorb in that environment cannot be taught in a classroom. The contacts you build, the standards you are exposed to, the problems you watch being solved at the highest level — these compound into an advantage that no qualification can replicate.

Do not let pride talk you out of a priceless opportunity. The student loan lasts for years. The experience lasts forever.

### Learn From the Best

If you learn from the second best, the best you can become is third.

This is not an exaggeration. The ceiling of your growth is set by the quality of the knowledge you absorb. Go after the best people in your field. Buy their books. Listen to their podcasts. Study how they think, how they speak, how they solve problems. If you can get close enough to offer them your service for free in exchange for access and mentorship, do it. That trade is worth more than most paid courses.

### Ask Yourself Every Night

Before you go to bed, ask yourself one question: *What have I learned today?*

If you cannot answer it, the day was not spent as well as it could have been. This single habit, practiced consistently, will compound into a level of mastery that surprises even you.

### Build Your Own Notes Bank

Keep a record of the most important things you learn. Not a passive diary — an active knowledge system. Write your notes as questions and answers. Revise them. Test yourself. Return to them.

Here is a simple system to start with:

1. **Capture:** When you learn something significant — in a meeting, from a book, from a failure, from feedback — write it down immediately.
2. **Convert:** Turn it into a question and answer. This forces you to understand it, not just record it.
3. **Revise:** Review your notes regularly. Use spare moments — commutes, waiting rooms, quiet evenings — to go back through them.
4. **Apply:** When you face a situation that matches something in your notes, use it. That is the moment your preparation becomes performance.

Over time, this system becomes a living record of your professional and personal growth. It is an asset that compounds quietly over years, and one day it will give you an edge that others cannot explain.

### Ask for Feedback — Always

After every significant challenge — an interview, a project, a presentation, a difficult conversation — ask for feedback. Even when it is uncomfortable. Especially when it is uncomfortable.

Most people do not ask because they are afraid of what they will hear. But the feedback that hurts is the feedback that helps. A gap you know about is a gap you can close. A gap you are hiding from will cost you the next opportunity.

Take the feedback. Separate the emotional sting from the practical signal. Add it to your notes. Then go and close the gap.

### The Standard: Be the Best

Preparing yourself to be the best is one of the most worthwhile investments you will ever make. Not because you will always win — you will not — but because the person who consistently prepares to compete at the highest level is the person who earns the right to be chosen when it counts.

Second best is not failure in every room. But in the rooms where only one person is selected, only one person gets paid. Make yourself that person.

### Use the Right Tool for the Right Job

Being the best is not just about knowledge and skill. It is about professionalism — showing up prepared, equipped, and ready for what the moment requires.

The wedding photographer who lost all his images was not a bad person. He simply came with the wrong equipment and no backup plan. His clients trusted him with something irreplaceable, and his lack of preparation cost them dearly.

Whatever your field, ask yourself before every important task: *Do I have the right tools? Have I prepared at the level this moment deserves?* Cutting corners on preparation is not efficiency — it is a debt you will pay later, usually at someone else's expense.

When you are entrusted with something that matters, your preparation is not just about you.

### When You Are the Best, People Will Wait for You

Genuine excellence is a form of power that no marketing can replicate.

The air-conditioning engineer I use in Nigeria is more expensive than anyone else available. He is often busy and sometimes hard to reach. And yet he is the only person I call. Not because I have no other options, I do but because I have learned, at cost, that the alternatives are not worth the risk.

When you reach that level of mastery in your field, something shifts. You stop chasing clients and clients start waiting for you. You stop competing on price and start commanding it. The people who know what good looks like will always choose quality over convenience.

That is worth working toward. Build the kind of reputation where the right people would rather wait for you than settle for someone else.
