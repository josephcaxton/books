# Requirements: Chapter 2 — The Practical Guide to Purpose

## Purpose
Chapter 2 bridges the personal memoir of Chapter 1 with actionable frameworks. It corrects the most common myths about purpose, proves through a real-world mentoring story that clarity follows action rather than precedes it, and equips the reader with three diagnostic questions and a practical exercise to find their own path.

---

## Content Requirements

### Requirement 1: The Lesson — Clarity Follows Action

**User Story:** As a reader, I want to understand the author's core insight about how purpose is found, so that I am freed from waiting for a perfect moment of certainty before I begin.

#### Acceptance Criteria

1. The lesson SHALL establish that purpose is a landscape mapped through action — not a hidden treasure found passively.
2. The author SHALL name the "autopilot" trap explicitly — people frozen by fear of making the wrong choice.
3. The core principle SHALL be stated clearly: clarity does not precede action, it follows it.
4. The tone SHALL be honest and retrospective — drawn from the author's own experience of wandering, not abstract theory.

---

### Requirement 2: The Core Distinctions

**User Story:** As a reader, I want to unlearn the myths I have been told about purpose, so that I can approach the search with a clearer, more realistic mindset.

#### Acceptance Criteria

1. The section SHALL contain exactly three distinctions, each with a bold heading.
2. **Purpose is Not Passion** SHALL be present — distinguishing emotional intensity (passion) from sustained direction (purpose).
3. **You Don't Need One Purpose Forever** SHALL be present — removing the paralysis of lifetime commitment.
4. **Clarity Comes From Engagement** SHALL be present — naming the "feel certain first" trap and correcting it.
5. Each distinction SHALL be explained in no more than three sentences.

---

### Requirement 3: The Mentee's Story

**User Story:** As a reader, I want to see real evidence that the chapter's principle works in practice, so that the advice feels grounded in lived experience rather than theory.

#### Acceptance Criteria

1. The mentee's account SHALL be quoted directly in his own words, clearly attributed as a real person the author mentors.
2. The quote SHALL describe his state before the mentoring session (overwhelmed, low confidence, holding onto past tragedy) and after (clarity, confidence, job offer).
3. The author's commentary SHALL follow the quote and explicitly decode the sequence: action came first, clarity followed.
4. The commentary SHALL name the core reversal: *feel ready → act* (the myth) vs. *act → feel ready* (the truth).
5. The section SHALL close with an aphoristic statement: *"Clarity is not a gift handed to the passive. It is a reward earned by the moving."*
6. The mentee's difficult circumstances (job loss, tragic events) SHALL be named to show the principle works even under pressure — not just in comfortable conditions.

---

### Requirement 4: The Three Questions

**User Story:** As a reader searching for direction, I want a set of honest, diagnostic questions to ask myself, so that I can identify the raw material of my purpose.

#### Acceptance Criteria

1. The section SHALL contain exactly three questions, each with a numbered heading.
2. **Question 1** SHALL ask what kinds of problems pull the reader's attention naturally — not what impresses others.
3. **Question 2** SHALL identify the sweet spot where competence, value to others, and psychological sustainability overlap.
4. **Question 3** SHALL ask what pain the reader is willing to endure — reframing the question from enjoyment to sustainable struggle.
5. Each question SHALL include a brief explanatory paragraph that makes it immediately actionable.

---

### Requirement 5: The Practical Exercise — Looking for the Tracks

**User Story:** As a reader, I want a concrete, written exercise I can do right now, so that I can begin mapping the tracks of my purpose immediately.

#### Acceptance Criteria

1. The exercise SHALL ask the reader to write down five specific things (moments of usefulness, aliveness, envy, deep emotion, and time-loss activities).
2. The reader SHALL be instructed to look for the common thread — the repetition across all five answers.
3. The exercise SHALL be framed as a "detective game" — purpose leaves tracks.
4. Instructions SHALL be written in second-person, direct address, making the reader feel immediately engaged.

---

### Requirement 6: The Final Note on Smaller Purposes

**User Story:** As a reader, I want reassurance that a meaningful life does not require public fame or extraordinary achievement, so that I do not dismiss my own path as insufficient.

#### Acceptance Criteria

1. The closing note SHALL explicitly reject the myth that meaningful purpose requires public fame.
2. It SHALL name specific "smaller" purposes (raising good children, mastering a craft, serving a community, building an honest business) as profound and sacred.
3. It SHALL end with an aphoristic closing line about small beginnings and a quiet, faithful life.
4. Tone SHALL be warm and affirming — not dismissive of ambition, but protective of those whose calling is quieter.

---

## Style Requirements

- First-person retrospective voice in Section 1 and the mentee story commentary.
- Direct second-person address ("you") in the advice sections and exercise.
- No banned clichés.
- The mentee quote must remain in his own words — do not paraphrase or smooth out his voice.
- Aphoristic closing lines required in the mentee story section and the final note.
